### Pattern Summary
My anxiety spikes when technical terminology appears in a professional context where I am expected to respond with authority. Approximately 67% of my fears appear manufactured — they reflect the Jargon Tollbooth in action: terms that sound like they should matter to my role but do not change any decision I make. The one real gap is practical: I have not yet used AI tools for the document drafting tasks I perform every week. The Adoption Mirage is present in incidents 1 and 2 — I assumed peers understood these terms and that I was falling behind, when the evidence suggests the terms were not operationally relevant to my role.

## Stage 2: Level Threshold

**Above my threshold (I need to understand):**
Output evaluation (can I trust this AI-generated clause or cost estimate?), workflow integration (where does AI fit in the RFP and evaluation process?), vendor claim assessment (is this AI capability real and relevant to my procurement context?), risk and compliance awareness (what are the failure modes that matter in public-sector contracting?).

**Below my threshold (someone else's job):**
Model architecture and training methods, embedding and vector database infrastructure, fine-tuning and prompt engineering at the engineering level, AI system deployment and maintenance, transformer internals.

**Justification:**
I drew my threshold at output evaluation and workflow integration because my two core job responsibilities are: (1) evaluating vendor proposals against public-sector compliance requirements, and (2) drafting and reviewing RFP scope sections and evaluation criteria. Both require me to judge the quality and reliability of outputs — whether from a vendor, a colleague, or an AI tool. Neither requires me to understand how those outputs were generated at a technical level. Believing otherwise is the Adoption Mirage: the assumption that because AI is involved, I need to understand all layers of the stack.

### Domain-Specific Failure Mode Map
Most dangerous hallucination scenario: An AI tool generates a compliance clause that cites a regulation incorrectly or references a superseded version of a procurement rule. In a public-sector context, this could invalidate a contract award or expose the organization to legal challenge. The error would be hard to detect because the clause would look syntactically correct and professionally written.

Hardest-to-detect error: An AI-assisted vendor scoring tool that reflects training data biases toward larger or more established vendors, systematically disadvantaging smaller suppliers in ways that are not visible in the scoring output.

Existing safeguards that would catch AI errors: Legal review of final contract documents; compliance checklist sign-off before award; conflict-of-interest declaration process.

Existing safeguards that would NOT catch AI errors: The compliance checklist does not verify that cited regulations are current versions. The scoring review process does not audit for systematic bias in AI-generated scores.

### Noise Ratio Analysis
High Signal: 3/10. Low or Deferred: 7/10. The ratio tells me that my professional information environment is dominated by vendor claims and hype rather than evidence-based signal. The three High Signal items are all grounded in direct observation or peer-reviewed evidence. The seven Low or Deferred items are vendor demos, conference presentations, and LinkedIn posts with no verifiable methodology.

Jargon Tollbooth term I am releasing: 'prompt engineering.' This term sits below my Level Threshold because my role does not require me to optimize AI system prompts at a technical level. What I actually need is the ability to write a clear, specific brief that produces a useful draft — which is a writing skill I already have, not a new engineering discipline.
